Discourse System Overview

What Are Discourse Markers?

Discourse markers are words and phrases that connect, organise, and signal the relationship between ideas in spoken and written communication. They are not just linking words you memorise for an exam — they are thinking tools that help both the speaker and the listener follow the logic of what is being said.

💡 Core Principle

Discourse markers are thinking tools, not vocabulary items. A student who uses them naturally is thinking in connected English — not stitching memorised phrases onto pre-planned ideas.

What Discourse Markers Actually Do
Organise Ideas
They give structure to speech and writing so that ideas flow in a logical order. Without them, communication can feel like disconnected facts.
Signal Relationships
They show HOW ideas connect — whether you are adding, contrasting, explaining, or concluding.
Help Real-Time Thinking
In speaking, markers like "well," "you know," and "I mean" give the speaker a moment to think while keeping the listener engaged.
Improve Listener Comprehension
They tell the listener what to expect next — "on the other hand" signals contrast before the contrast even arrives.
The Key Distinction
❌ Memorised Linking Words

"Furthermore, it is undeniable that… Moreover, this demonstrates that…"

Sounds mechanical. The student is performing the language, not using it.

✅ Functional Communication Tools

"And I think the main reason is… Actually, what's interesting is… I mean, when you think about it…"

Sounds natural. The language follows the thinking, not the other way around.

What Additive Markers Do

Additive markers add information — they say "here is something else that supports, expands, or extends what I just said." They represent cumulative thinking: stacking evidence, examples, and ideas to build a stronger point.

A speaker uses additive markers when they have more than one thing to say and want the listener to understand all of it belongs together. Without them, two connected ideas may sound like unrelated statements.

⚠️ Why "furthermore" sounds wrong in speech
Native speakers almost never say furthermore in conversation. It signals that the speaker is reading from a script rather than thinking spontaneously. In IELTS Speaking, this lowers your fluency band score because it sounds rehearsed.
🎤 Natural Speaking Patterns
  • I think ___, and also, ___.
  • Another thing is ___. That's actually quite important.
  • On top of that, ___.
  • And what's more, ___.
  • Not only that, but ___.
  • Apart from that, I'd say ___.
✅ Natural IELTS Part 3 Answer
"I think social media has changed the way we communicate, and also, it's affected how we get our news. On top of that, it's really changed the way young people interact socially. So there are quite a few dimensions to it."
✍️ Writing Patterns (Task 2)
  • In addition to this, ___.
  • Furthermore, it can be argued that ___.
  • Moreover, the evidence suggests that ___.
  • A further consideration is that ___.
✅ Band 7–9 Writing Example
"Urban green spaces improve the mental wellbeing of city residents. Furthermore, they provide vital ecosystems for local wildlife, which is increasingly threatened by urban expansion."
⚠️ Common Errors
Error 1 · Using writing markers in speech
❌ "Moreover, I strongly believe that…"
✅ "I also think that…" / "And what's more, I believe…"
Error 2 · Overusing "also"
❌ "I like sport. I also like music. I also enjoy cooking. I also…"
✅ Vary: "I like sport. On top of that, music is a big part of my life. And I enjoy cooking, too."
Error 3 · Mechanical chaining
❌ "Firstly… Secondly… Furthermore… Additionally… Moreover…"
✅ Mix structures and patterns rather than stacking formal markers.
What Apposition Markers Do

Apposition markers clarify or develop a point by either exemplifying (giving an example) or restating (saying the same thing in different words). They answer the listener's silent question: "Can you give me an example?" or "What exactly do you mean?"

These are among the most important markers for IELTS Speaking because the examiner is explicitly looking for your ability to develop and extend your answers.

Subtypes: Exemplification vs Restatement
Exemplification

Giving a concrete example to support a general claim.

for example / for instance / take ___ for example / like / such as
Restatement

Rewording a complex idea to make it clearer.

in other words / I mean / what I mean is / that is / basically / put simply

🎤 Natural Speaking Patterns
  • ___, like ___. That's a good example of what I mean.
  • Take ___ for example — it's actually a really good illustration of ___.
  • I mean, what I'm saying is ___.
  • What I mean is, ___ — basically, it comes down to ___.
  • ___, such as ___ or ___.
  • In other words, ___.
✅ Natural IELTS Part 2 Response
"I'd say the most significant change is how we stay connected — I mean, before smartphones, if you were out of the house, no one could reach you. Take my parents, for example — they had to use payphones. In other words, communication used to require a lot more effort."
✍️ Writing Patterns (Task 2)
  • For example, ___.
  • For instance, a recent study found that ___.
  • This can be illustrated by ___.
  • That is to say, ___.
  • In other words, ___.
  • To illustrate this point, ___.
✅ Band 7–9 Writing Example
"Many governments have struggled to implement effective climate policy. For instance, several nations that signed the Paris Agreement have failed to meet their initial emissions targets, demonstrating that international commitments do not automatically translate into domestic action."
⚠️ Common Errors
Error 1 · Vague examples
❌ "For example, some people do this."
✅ Make it specific: "Take Japan, for example — the government introduced a four-day work week policy…"
Error 2 · Restating without developing
❌ "It is bad. In other words, it is not good."
✅ Add new information when restating — don't just repeat the same idea with different words.
What Consequential Markers Do

Consequential markers express logical relationships of cause, result, and condition. They tell the listener why something happens or what happens as a result. This category is central to analytical thinking — and IELTS rewards candidates who can explain, not just describe.

Cause

because, since, as, due to, owing to

Result

so, therefore, as a result, consequently, this means that, that's why

Condition

if, unless, provided that, as long as, in that case

🎤 Natural Speaking Patterns
  • ___, so ___. That's basically why ___.
  • The main reason is that ___. I mean, if ___, then ___.
  • It happens because ___. And that's why ___.
  • As a result, ___ — which makes sense when you think about it.
  • ___, which means that ___.
  • If ___, then obviously ___.
✅ Natural IELTS Part 3 Answer
"I think people are moving to cities mainly because there are more job opportunities there. So naturally, rural areas end up losing their younger population, which means that local communities start to decline. And if governments don't step in, that process becomes really difficult to reverse."
✍️ Writing Patterns (Task 2)
  • As a result of ___, ___.
  • This has led to ___, which consequently ___.
  • Due to ___, many ___ have experienced ___.
  • Therefore, it can be argued that ___.
  • Provided that ___, ___ could be achievable.
✅ Band 7–9 Writing Example
"As global temperatures continue to rise, coastal ecosystems face increasing degradation. Consequently, millions of people who depend on fisheries for their livelihoods may experience severe economic hardship in the coming decades."
⚠️ Common Errors
Error 1 · "Therefore" in speech
❌ "Therefore, I strongly believe that…"
✅ "So I think…" / "That's why I believe…"
Error 2 · Confusing cause and result
❌ "Because of pollution, this is the reason." (unclear logic)
✅ Be precise: "Pollution causes health problems, and as a result, healthcare costs rise."
What Comparison Markers Do

Comparison markers draw similarities between two ideas, situations, or things. They signal that the speaker is thinking in parallel — seeing a connection between different things. This type of thinking demonstrates analytical depth and is valued in both IELTS Speaking and Writing.

Key markers: similarly, likewise, in the same way, equally, by the same token, just as

🎤 Natural Speaking Patterns

  • Just like ___, ___ also ___.
  • ___, and similarly, ___.
  • You could say the same about ___.
  • In the same way, ___.
✅ Natural IELTS Answer
"I think children learn languages much faster than adults — it's the same with music, actually. Just like language, musical training is much more effective when it starts early."
✍️ Writing Patterns (Task 2)
  • Similarly, ___.
  • Likewise, ___ has also been shown to ___.
  • In the same way that ___, ___ also ___.
  • This parallels the situation in ___, where ___.
⚠️ Common Errors
Error · Using comparison markers when contrasting
❌ "Similarly, online education is worse than traditional classrooms."
✅ Use contrast markers when ideas differ — comparison markers only for genuine similarities.
What Contrastive Markers Do

Contrastive markers express opposition, exception, or alternatives between ideas. They are among the most commonly tested markers in IELTS because expressing nuanced disagreement and counter-argument is a core skill at Band 7+.

Four Subtypes Explained
SubtypeMeaningKey MarkersExample
AntitheticDirect, clear opposition — "A is true, B is the opposite"but, however, whereas, while, on the other handSome prefer cities; others prefer the countryside.
ConcessiveAcknowledging a point but reducing its strength — "Yes, but…"although, even though, despite, nevertheless, grantedAlthough it has benefits, the drawbacks are significant.
ReformulatoryCorrecting or adjusting what was just saidactually, I mean, rather, or rather, wellIt's expensive — or rather, it's not affordable for most people.
ReplaciveOffering an alternative insteadinstead, alternatively, rather than, on the contraryInstead of banning cars, cities could expand cycling infrastructure.
Nuance: "although" vs "even though" vs "despite"

although+ clauseNeutral concessionAlthough it is expensive, many people still buy it.
even though+ clauseStronger — the speaker is surprisedEven though it is very expensive, people still buy it.
despite+ noun/gerundFormal, factual concessionDespite the high price, people continue to buy it.
in spite of+ noun/gerundSame as despite — slightly more formalIn spite of the high price, demand has remained strong.

🎤 Natural Speaking Patterns
  • I see your point, but at the same time, ___.
  • Some people think ___, whereas I think ___.
  • ___, though. That's an important distinction.
  • Even though ___, ___.
  • Well, actually, ___. Or rather, ___.
  • Instead of ___, I think it would be better to ___.
✅ Natural IELTS Part 3 Answer
"I think technology has improved our lives in many ways. At the same time, though, there are definitely some downsides. Even though we're more connected than ever, a lot of people actually feel more isolated. So it's not really a simple picture — it depends a lot on how you use it."
✍️ Writing Patterns (Task 2)
  • While ___, ___.
  • Although ___, it can be argued that ___.
  • Despite ___, many argue that ___.
  • However, there are those who contend that ___.
  • On the other hand, ___.
  • Nevertheless, the evidence suggests that ___.
✅ Band 7–9 Writing Example
"While economic growth has historically depended on industrial expansion, it can be argued that sustainable development offers a more viable long-term model. Despite the higher initial costs, renewable energy infrastructure consistently proves cost-effective over a 20-year horizon."
⚠️ Common Errors
Error 1 · "However" overuse in writing
❌ Using "however" to begin every counter-argument paragraph.
✅ Vary: While / Although / Even so / That said / Nevertheless
Error 2 · "But" only in speaking
❌ "But, the government should not…" (in a formal essay)
✅ Use "However" or "Nevertheless" in writing; keep "but" for speech.
Error 3 · Wrong structure with "despite"
❌ "Despite the fact, many people…"
✅ "Despite the fact that…" OR "Despite + noun phrase"

  • Particularly noteworthy is ___.
  • Broadly speaking, ___.
  • It is worth noting that, above all, ___.

⚠️ Common Errors
Error · Overusing "obviously"
❌ "Obviously, the government should… Obviously, people want… Obviously, this shows…"
✅ Use sparingly — it can sound dismissive or condescending if overused.
Error · Vague generalisations
❌ "In general, things are getting better."
✅ Follow generalisations with a specific qualifier: "In general, access to education has improved — though significant regional disparities remain."
What Sequential Markers Do

Sequential markers organise ideas in time or logical order. They are essential for IELTS Speaking Part 2, where you must structure a two-minute monologue clearly. They help the listener track where you are in your response.

✅ IELTS Part 2 Structure (2-minute cue card)
"Right, so the person I'd like to talk about is my secondary school teacher, Mr. Santos. To begin with, I should explain where I met him — it was actually in my third year of school. Then, what I remember most is how he used to explain things — he had this really engaging way of teaching. Eventually, I realised he'd had a huge impact on how I think. And looking back, I'd say he's one of the main reasons I became interested in learning."
✍️ Writing Patterns (Task 2)
  • First and foremost, ___.
  • Subsequently, ___.
  • In the first instance, ___. This is followed by ___.
  • As a final point, ___.
⚠️ Common Errors
Error 1 · Overusing "firstly… secondly… thirdly…"
❌ Every IELTS essay beginning with: "Firstly… Secondly… Thirdly… Finally…"
✅ Vary your sequencing language and embed it naturally into sentences.
Error 2 · "Subsequently" in IELTS Speaking
❌ "Subsequently, I decided to…"
✅ "After that, I decided to…" / "Then I thought, well…"
What Summative Markers Do

Summative markers signal that the speaker is wrapping up a sequence of ideas and drawing a conclusion. They are used to synthesise everything that has been said into a single, clear takeaway.

In IELTS Speaking, effective use of summative markers shows that the candidate can manage longer turns and bring them to a coherent close — which directly affects your Coherence & Cohesion score.

Key markers: overall, in conclusion, so basically, all in all, to sum up, on balance, in short, when all is said and done, the bottom line is

⚠️ "In conclusion" in speaking
Saying "In conclusion" in a speaking test sounds extremely unnatural — like reading the end of an essay. Use "So basically…" or "All in all…" instead.
🎤 Natural Speaking Patterns
  • So basically, ___ — that's my main point.
  • All in all, I'd say ___.
  • The bottom line is ___.
  • When all is said and done, ___.
  • Overall, though, I think ___.
  • So, to bring it all together, ___.
✅ Natural Part 3 Close
"So basically, I think the biggest issue is that we treat these problems separately when they're actually interconnected. All in all, what we really need is a more holistic approach — one that looks at the social, economic, and environmental dimensions together."
✍️ Writing Patterns (Task 2)
  • In conclusion, it can be argued that ___.
  • To summarise, ___.
  • On balance, the evidence suggests that ___.
  • Overall, while ___, the preponderance of evidence indicates ___.
⚠️ Common Errors
Error 1 · "In conclusion, I have discussed…" (summary, not synthesis)
❌ Repeating what you said rather than drawing a meaningful conclusion.
✅ State a clear position or leave the reader with a thought-provoking idea.
Error 2 · Mid-answer summary
❌ Saying "So basically…" too early, before you've fully developed the point.
✅ Summative markers should come at the END of a complete idea — not as an escape from developing it further.

Section 3

Why Good Writing ≠ Good Speaking

The Core Difference

Writing and speaking are two fundamentally different modes of communication. A student who writes well does not automatically speak well — and vice versa. This distinction is critical for IELTS, which tests both modes separately.

🎤 Speaking Is…

Real-time, spontaneous, flexible, and conversational. It allows for hesitation, self-correction, and natural filler markers (well, you know, I mean). It rewards naturalness, not formality.

✍️ Writing Is…

Planned, formal, structured, and controlled. It rewards precision, cohesion, and sophisticated vocabulary — but these same qualities sound unnatural when spoken aloud.

What This Means for IELTS Band Score

The IELTS Speaking band descriptors reward natural language use. A candidate who uses overly formal markers consistently will be marked down for lack of fluency and naturalness — even if the grammar is correct.

Conversely, in Writing Task 2, informal spoken markers (so, and, but, basically) at the start of every sentence will reduce your Lexical Resource and Coherence scores.

💡 The Rule of Thumb

If it sounds like something you'd write in a formal essay, don't say it. If it sounds like something a friend would say, don't write it in Task 2.

Section 4

IELTS Application

Speaking Part 2 — Organising a Long Answer

Part 2 requires a 1–2 minute monologue. Discourse markers are what make the difference between a list of facts and a coherent, developed response.

✅ Model Part 2 Structure

Open: "Right, so the topic I've been given is about a place I enjoy visiting…"

Describe: "To begin with, I should say that this place is actually quite close to where I grew up…"

Develop: "What makes it special is… On top of that… I mean, what I find most interesting is…"

Conclude: "So all in all, I'd say it holds a lot of personal significance for me."

Speaking Part 3 — Discussion & Opinion

Part 3 is a discussion where you need to express opinions, agree/disagree, speculate, and explore ideas. Discourse markers are essential for all of these functions.

📌 Final Reminder for IELTS Learners

Discourse markers only improve your score when they are used correctly and naturally. A response with three well-placed, genuinely connected markers will always outperform one that mechanically inserts ten formal linking words.

Your goal is not to use discourse markers — your goal is to think with them.
