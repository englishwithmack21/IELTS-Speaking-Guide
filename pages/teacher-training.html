Module 01

The Architecture of the IELTS Speaking Test

Understanding the structure, purpose, and design logic of the test so teachers can train students strategically.

The IELTS Speaking test is not simply a conversation. It is a structured interview designed to measure specific communicative abilities across three progressively demanding sections. Each part evaluates different aspects of spoken communication, assessed using four criteria: Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation.

Learning Objectives
  1. Explain the structure and timing of the IELTS Speaking test.
  2. Identify the purpose of each part of the test.
  3. Understand how examiners conduct the interview.
  4. Recognize how the test progressively increases in difficulty.
  5. Simulate the speaking test accurately in the classroom.
Core Knowledge

Overview of the IELTS Speaking Test

The IELTS Speaking test is a face-to-face interview between the candidate and an examiner. The total duration is approximately 11–14 minutes. The examiner follows a standardized script to ensure fairness and consistency across candidates.

PartDurationFocus
Part 14–5 minutesPersonal questions
Part 23–4 minutesIndividual long turn
Part 34–5 minutesAbstract discussion

Part 1 — Introduction and Personal Questions

In this section, the examiner asks candidates about familiar topics such as hometown, work or studies, hobbies, daily routines, food, and travel. These questions help candidates relax and assess basic conversational ability. Candidates should give natural answers of one or two sentences rather than single-word responses.

Part 2 — The Long Turn

Candidates receive a cue card with a topic and are given 1 minute to prepare and 1–2 minutes to speak. This section measures fluency, narrative ability, and the ability to organize ideas.

Example Cue Card

Describe a memorable trip you took.

  • where you went
  • who you went with
  • what you did there
  • and explain why it was memorable.

Part 3 — Two-Way Discussion

This section expands on the Part 2 topic with more abstract and analytical questions. Part 3 measures the candidate's ability to express opinions, justify arguments, compare ideas, and speculate about the future. This section often determines whether a student can reach a higher band.

Progressive Difficulty of the Test

SectionCognitive Demand
Part 1Personal information
Part 2Personal storytelling
Part 3Abstract discussion

Core Knowledge

The Four IELTS Speaking Assessment Criteria

Each criterion is scored individually, and the final speaking band score is the average of the four scores.

CriterionFocus
Fluency and CoherenceFlow and organization of speech
Lexical ResourceRange and accuracy of vocabulary
Grammatical Range and AccuracyVariety and correctness of grammar
PronunciationClarity and intelligibility of speech

Criterion 1 — Fluency and Coherence

Fluency refers to how smoothly and naturally a candidate speaks. Coherence refers to how logically ideas are organized and connected. Common indicators of weak fluency include frequent pauses, repetition, hesitation, and self-correction.

Criterion 2 — Lexical Resource

Examiners evaluate whether a candidate can use appropriate vocabulary, paraphrase effectively, and avoid excessive repetition. Higher band scores require flexible vocabulary use, natural collocations, and the ability to express nuanced ideas.

Criterion 3 — Grammatical Range and Accuracy

This criterion evaluates both the variety and accuracy of grammar structures. It is important to note that minor grammar mistakes do not automatically reduce the score if communication remains clear.

Criterion 4 — Pronunciation

Pronunciation is assessed based on intelligibility — the ability to communicate clearly. Examiners evaluate word stress, sentence stress, rhythm, intonation, and linking sounds. Accents are acceptable; clarity is more important than perfection.

How the Final Speaking Score Is Calculated

Scoring Example
CriterionScore
Fluency and Coherence6
Lexical Resource7
Grammar6
Pronunciation6

(6 + 7 + 6 + 6) ÷ 4 = 6.25 → Rounded to Band 6.5
Note: In JIC, this becomes 6.0 as scores are rounded down to the nearest half band.

Core Knowledge

The Diagnostic Cycle

Observe → Diagnose → Train → Reassess This cycle allows teachers to identify root causes of speaking difficulties rather than correcting isolated mistakes.

The Four Types of Speaking Problems

Problem TypeDescription
Fluency ProblemsHesitation, pauses, repetition
Vocabulary ProblemsLimited lexical range
Grammar ProblemsIncorrect sentence structures
Pronunciation ProblemsDifficulty being understood

Fluency Problems

Common indicators include long pauses, filler words ("uh", "um"), repetition, false starts, and self-corrections. Students with fluency problems may know the answer but cannot express ideas smoothly.

Vocabulary Problems

Common signs include repeating the same words frequently, using basic vocabulary, and difficulty paraphrasing.

Example

"I like traveling because it is very good and very nice."

The student has the idea but lacks the vocabulary to express it effectively.

Grammar Problems

Typical indicators include incorrect verb tenses, missing articles, incorrect word order, and overuse of simple sentences. Teachers should identify whether the issue is accuracy (errors) or range (limited sentence structures).

Pronunciation Problems

Common issues include incorrect word stress, unclear consonant sounds, monotone intonation, and syllable omission. Accent is not a problem as long as speech remains intelligible.

Teaching Methodology

Separating Thinking Problems from Language Problems

Problem TypeExample
Thinking ProblemStudent does not know what to say
Language ProblemStudent knows the idea but cannot express it

If the problem is idea generation, provide input and brainstorming activities. If the problem is language expression, focus on vocabulary and grammar development.

The Input → Processing → Output Model

To solve the idea-generation problem, teachers can use a three-stage speaking model.

StagePurposeExamples
InputProvides content knowledgeReadings, statistics, images, opinion statements, videos

Idea development is directly related to Fluency and Coherence. Students who cannot generate ideas often pause frequently, give incomplete answers, and struggle to maintain fluency. Improving idea generation therefore improves fluency scores.

Core Knowledge

Why Students Need Idea Frameworks

Speaking in a second language requires students to simultaneously generate ideas, organize them, choose vocabulary, and construct sentences. Idea frameworks reduce cognitive load by giving students a clear pattern to follow.

Types of IELTS Speaking Questions

Question TypeExample
OpinionDo you think public transportation should be free?
ExperienceDescribe a memorable trip.
ComparisonHow is life different now compared to the past?
PredictionHow might technology change education in the future?

Framework 1 — ORE (Opinion → Reason → Example)

Most useful for Part 1 and Part 3 questions.

Example Response

Q: Do you think exercise is important?

Opinion: Yes, I believe exercise is very important…
Reason: …because it helps people maintain good physical and mental health.
Example: For example, regular exercise can reduce stress and improve overall fitness.

Framework 2 — Advantages and Disadvantages

Works well for Part 3 discussion questions.

Example Response

Q: What are the advantages and disadvantages of online education?

One advantage is flexibility… However, a disadvantage is that some students may feel isolated without face-to-face interaction.

Framework 3 — Cause and Effect

Useful for analytical questions in Part 3.

Structure: Cause → Effect → Example

Many people move to big cities because they offer better job opportunities. As a result, cities often experience rapid population growth.

Framework 4 — Past / Present / Future

Works well for comparison and prediction questions.

Example Response

In the past, most people traveled by train or bus. Today, many people prefer private cars or airplanes. In the future, we may see more electric and autonomous vehicles.

Framework 5 — Storytelling Framework (Part 2)

StagePurpose
SituationWhere the event took place
EventsWhat happened during the experience
FeelingsWhy it was memorable
ReflectionWhat you learned from the experience
Key Principle Frameworks should be presented as thinking tools, not rigid formulas. Students should understand the structure and adapt it naturally during speaking.

Expected Answer Length

Part 1 answers should typically be two to three sentences. Very short answers may indicate weak fluency.

Weak vs. Strong Answer

Weak: Yes, I like traveling.

Stronger: Yes, I really enjoy traveling because it allows me to experience different cultures. For example, when I visited Japan, I was fascinated by the food and traditional architecture.

Teaching Methodology

The Answer → Reason → Example / Detail Structure

Expansion Pattern

Answer: Yes, I enjoy reading…
Reason: …because it helps me relax after a long day.

Corrective Feedback Techniques

1. Recasting — Model the correct form

Student: She go to school every day.
Teacher: Yes, she goes to school every day.

2. Elicitation — Prompt self-correction

Teacher: She…?    Student: She goes to school every day.

3. Metalinguistic Feedback — Name the error type

Student: He go yesterday.    Teacher: Past tense?    Student: He went yesterday.

Classroom Application

Delayed Feedback Board Activity

During speaking activities, the teacher writes common errors on the board without identifying the student. Students then work together to identify and correct the errors. This encourages collaborative correction without causing embarrassment.

Activity 1 — Rapid Question Drill

Teacher asks students a series of short questions. Students must respond immediately with 2–3 sentences. This improves response speed and conversational fluency.

Activity 2 — Timed Speaking

Students answer a question for progressively longer periods: 30 seconds → 60 seconds → 90 seconds. This activity helps students sustain speech for longer periods of time.

Activity 3 — Cue Card Simulation

Teacher gives students cue cards similar to those used in the IELTS exam. Students have 1 minute to prepare and 2 minutes to speak.

Sample Cue Card

Describe a place you enjoy visiting.

  • where it is
  • what you do there
  • who you go with

Activity 4 — Opinion Debate

Teacher presents a statement. Students form two groups (agree / disagree) and present arguments with examples. This develops reasoning skills and vocabulary for argumentation.

Activity 5 — Idea Expansion

Teacher asks a question. Students must include: one reason · one example · one explanation. This helps students develop more detailed responses.

Activity 6 — Partner Interview

Students interview each other using IELTS-style questions, then report their partner's answers to the class. This develops interactive speaking skills and active listening.

Step-by-Step Mock Test Procedure

Step 1 — Introduction

Examiner Script "Good afternoon. My name is [Teacher's Name]. I will be your examiner today."
Ask: What is your full name? / Where are you from?

Step 2 — Part 1 Questions

Ask questions about familiar topics. Cover two or three related topic areas.

Step 3 — Part 2 Cue Card

Instruction to Candidate "You will have one minute to prepare your answer. You may take notes if you wish."
After one minute, the student speaks for 1–2 minutes. Do not interrupt unless the student stops.

Step 4 — Part 3 Discussion

Ask questions related to the Part 2 topic. Encourage extended discussion and reasoning.

Providing Feedback After the Test

Feedback should focus on all four speaking criteria. Highlight strengths, areas for improvement, and specific strategies for development. Feedback should be specific and constructive, never vague.
