Foundation

What Is Part 3? A Clear, Practical Overview

What happens in Part 3?

Part 3 is a two-way discussion between you and the examiner. It lasts approximately 4–5 minutes. The examiner asks you abstract, opinion-based questions that are connected to the topic of your Part 2 talk. You are expected to speak in depth, explain your reasoning, compare ideas, and discuss broader societal issues.

Unlike a simple interview, Part 3 is a genuine exchange of ideas. The examiner may follow up on your answers, ask you to clarify, or push deeper into a topic.

How Part 3 Differs from Parts 1 and 2

Feature Part 1 Part 2 Part 3
Format Short Q&A Solo monologue (2 min) Two-way discussion
Topics Personal / familiar Personal experience Abstract / societal
Answer length 2–4 sentences ~2 minutes 30–60 seconds per question
Thinking required Minimal Moderate High — ideas + reasoning
Tone Conversational Narrative Analytical / discursive
Key challenge Fluency Organisation Depth + flexibility

Why Part 3 Questions Feel More Difficult

Many learners find Part 3 challenging for three main reasons:

  • Abstract topics — Questions often involve society, trends, values, and concepts rather than personal experiences.
  • Open-ended thinking — There is no single correct answer. You must form and defend an opinion in real time.
  • Extended responses expected — Short answers of one or two sentences are not enough. You need to explain, justify, and develop your ideas.
💡
The good news

You do not need to be an expert on any topic. What matters is that you can think out loud, give reasons, and express ideas clearly. This guide gives you a system for doing exactly that.

The Core Skill: Expanding Ideas Using Patterns

The single most important skill in Part 3 is the ability to take a simple idea and expand it into a clear, developed answer. This is done not by memorising long sentences, but by following flexible thinking patterns — logical sequences that guide what to say next.

Think of each pattern as a thought path: once you have your first idea, the pattern tells you exactly how to build on it. Over time, these patterns become natural instincts — not templates you recite, but habits of thinking that make extended speaking feel easy.

What patterns give you

  • A clear direction when you start talking
  • Logical ways to add more content
  • Natural sounding answers (not memorised scripts)
  • Confidence to handle unfamiliar questions

What patterns are NOT

  • Sentences you memorise word for word
  • Rigid templates that limit your ideas
  • Essay-writing structures
  • A guarantee of a specific answer

Core Framework

Core Speaking Patterns — The Heart of the Guide

The ten patterns below are the foundation of this system. Each one gives you a thinking sequence — a series of steps that guides what you say next. Study each pattern carefully, practise with the examples, and then begin using them flexibly in your own answers.

📌
How to use these patterns

Do not try to memorise the exact wording of any example. Instead, understand the logic of each pattern — the sequence of steps. Then practise applying that logic to new questions.

P1

Direct Answer → Reason → Example

When to use it

Best for opinion questions, factual questions, or any question with a clear "yes/no/I think…" answer. This is your most-used, go-to pattern.

[State your position or answer clearly] + [Give the main reason] + [Support it with a real or hypothetical example]

Examples

Question: Do you think children spend too much time on screens?
I do think it's a growing concern. The main reason is that children are losing opportunities for physical play and face-to-face interaction, which are critical for their development. For example, studies in several countries have shown that children who spend more than four hours a day on screens tend to report higher levels of anxiety and lower social skills by the time they reach their teens.
Question: Is it important for companies to offer flexible working hours?
Absolutely, I think it's become essential. The reason is that people have very different productivity rhythms — some people focus best early in the morning, others late at night. A good example is the shift in many tech companies, where flexible hours have actually led to higher output and lower staff turnover.
P2

Opinion → Explanation → Result

When to use it

Use when you need to show not just what you think, but why it matters. This pattern forces you to think about consequences.

[Express your view] + [Explain the reasoning or mechanism behind it] + [Describe the likely outcome or consequence]

Examples

Question: Should environmental education be compulsory in schools?
I strongly believe it should be. If young people understand how ecosystems work and how human behaviour affects them, they're far more likely to make sustainable choices as adults. The result would be a generation that treats environmental responsibility as a normal part of life, rather than a political issue.
Question: Do you think governments should fund public art?
I think they should, to a reasonable degree. When public spaces are visually engaging and culturally rich, they encourage people to spend more time outdoors and connect with their community. Over time, this can genuinely strengthen a sense of shared identity and civic pride.
P3

General Statement → Specific Example

When to use it

Great for answering questions about trends, society, or general habits. You make a broad claim, then immediately support it with a concrete example.

[Make a general observation about the topic] + [Support it with a specific example, statistic, or scenario]

Examples

Question: How has social media changed the way people communicate?
In general, social media has made communication much faster but arguably shallower. A clear example is the shift from long emails or phone calls to short messages and emojis — we respond instantly, but we often share very little real meaning. A lot of nuance gets lost in that process.
Question: Is reading still important in the digital age?
Reading remains one of the most valuable skills anyone can develop. Take, for instance, the ability to understand complex instructions, evaluate news critically, or simply enjoy a long-form narrative — none of that is replaced by video content. If anything, the digital world demands stronger reading skills because we're bombarded with so much written information every day.
P4

Compare → Explain Difference → Give Reason

When to use it

Ideal for comparison questions, or whenever you want to contrast two approaches, groups, or time periods to make a point.

[Identify two things being compared] + [Describe how they differ] + [Explain why the difference exists]

Examples

Question: How do older and younger generations view work differently?
There's quite a significant contrast. Older generations, broadly speaking, tend to prioritise job security and loyalty to one employer, whereas younger workers often prefer variety, flexibility, and purpose over stability. I think this difference comes from the economic environments they grew up in — older generations experienced recessions that made security feel paramount, while younger people have grown up seeing rapid change and have adapted accordingly.
Question: How is public transport different in cities and rural areas?
In urban areas, public transport tends to be frequent, affordable, and integrated, while in rural settings it's often minimal or non-existent. The reason is largely economic — cities have the population density to make investment viable, whereas rural areas simply can't generate enough demand to justify the cost.
P5

Cause → Effect → Real-World Example

When to use it

Perfect for cause and effect questions, or when explaining how one thing leads to another — a very common pattern in Part 3 discussions about social issues.

[Identify what causes something] + [Explain what happens as a result] + [Anchor it with a real or plausible example]

Examples

Question: What causes people to feel stressed about work?
I think one of the biggest causes is the blurring of boundaries between work and personal life, particularly since remote working became common. When you can receive emails at any hour, it becomes very hard to truly switch off. As a result, many people report feeling constantly "on call," which builds into chronic stress over time. We can see this clearly in countries like Japan and South Korea, where overwork culture has become a serious public health conversation.
Question: What are the effects of urbanisation on communities?
Rapid urbanisation often weakens local community bonds. When people move to cities for work, they frequently don't know their neighbours, lack local support networks, and can feel quite isolated despite being surrounded by millions of people. Cities like London or Tokyo have very high population densities, yet research consistently shows high rates of loneliness among their residents.
P6

Advantage → Explanation → Result

When to use it

Use for advantage/disadvantage questions, or when you want to argue in favour of something clearly and persuasively.

[State the main benefit] + [Explain why or how it provides that benefit] + [Describe the positive outcome]

Examples

Question: What are the advantages of learning a second language?
One of the clearest advantages is cognitive flexibility. When you constantly switch between languages, your brain becomes better at filtering out irrelevant information and managing complex tasks. As a result, bilingual individuals tend to perform better in problem-solving scenarios and show stronger concentration overall.
Question: What are the benefits of living in a city?
Access to opportunity is probably the most significant advantage. Cities concentrate jobs, universities, hospitals, and cultural institutions in one place. The result is that people in urban areas generally have more choices — in terms of careers, education, and lifestyle — which can significantly improve their quality of life.
P7

Problem → Solution → Outcome

When to use it

Excellent for questions about social problems, challenges, or what should be done about an issue.

[Describe the problem clearly] + [Propose a realistic solution] + [Explain the expected positive outcome]

Examples

Question: How can governments reduce traffic congestion in cities?
The core problem is that private car use is simply too convenient compared to alternatives. One effective solution would be investing heavily in affordable, reliable public transport while simultaneously making car ownership more expensive through congestion charges. If implemented well, cities would see fewer vehicles on the road, reduced pollution, and commuters who actually arrive at work less stressed.
Question: What can be done to address youth unemployment?
The central issue is often a skills mismatch — young people graduate with qualifications that don't match what employers actually need. A solution that's worked well in places like Germany is investing in vocational training and apprenticeship programmes that connect education directly to industry. The outcome is graduates who are immediately employable, and businesses that spend less time and money training new hires from scratch.
P8

Present → Past Comparison → Reason

When to use it

Use when a question asks you to compare how things have changed, or when you want to show that something is better or worse than before.

[Describe the current situation] + [Contrast it with how things were in the past] + [Explain what caused the change]

Examples

Question: How have attitudes towards mental health changed?
Today, there's much more openness around mental health — people are far more willing to seek help and talk about their experiences. This is quite different from even 20 years ago, when mental illness was heavily stigmatised and many people suffered in silence. I think the change has come from a combination of celebrity figures speaking publicly about their struggles, and campaigns by health organisations that gradually shifted the narrative.
Question: Is shopping today different from how it was in the past?
Shopping now is almost unrecognisably different. Today, people expect to buy anything online, receive it within 24 hours, and return it easily if needed. A generation ago, shopping meant physically visiting a store, comparing limited options, and waiting weeks for special orders. The shift has been driven almost entirely by technology and the expectations consumers have developed from it.
P9

Present → Future Prediction → Justification

When to use it

Essential for speculative and future-oriented questions. This pattern shows the examiner you can reason about what might happen and why.

[Describe the current state of affairs] + [Make a clear prediction about the future] + [Justify why you believe this will happen]

Examples

Question: How do you think AI will affect employment in the future?
Currently, AI is automating routine tasks in industries like manufacturing and data processing. I think in the next decade we'll likely see significant disruption in roles that involve repetitive cognitive work — things like data entry, basic customer service, and even some aspects of accounting. The justification is simple: if a machine can do a job faster and more accurately at a lower cost, companies will use it. That said, I'd expect new jobs to emerge in areas we can't fully predict yet.
Question: Will traditional newspapers survive in the digital age?
Right now, print journalism is clearly struggling — circulation is falling and many papers have moved online. My prediction is that print will survive but in a much smaller, niche form — perhaps luxury weekend editions or highly specialised publications for professional readers. The reason is that some audiences still place high value on the experience of physical reading, and a well-designed newspaper can feel like a premium product.
P10

Idea → Counterpoint → Balanced Conclusion

When to use it

Use this when you want to show critical thinking. Presenting both sides of an issue demonstrates maturity of thought and avoids one-dimensional answers.

[Present your main idea or one side] + [Acknowledge a valid counterpoint] + [Offer a balanced, nuanced conclusion]

Examples

Question: Should university education be free for everyone?
There's a strong argument that education is a public good — an educated population benefits society as a whole, so the state should invest in it. However, free university education isn't without its complications. If it's entirely free, there may be less accountability for the quality of programmes, and limited funding could affect resources. On balance, I think a model where fees are low or income-based, but quality is protected, is probably more realistic than completely free education for all.
Question: Is it better to live in a city or in the countryside?
Cities clearly offer more professional and social opportunities, and for younger people especially, that matters enormously. On the other hand, rural living offers space, cleaner air, and often a stronger sense of community that's very hard to find in a big city. Ultimately, I think it depends entirely on your stage of life — cities suit people building careers, while the countryside may suit families or those seeking a slower pace.

Application

Question Types & Which Patterns to Use

Part 3 questions fall into several recognisable types. Learning to identify the question type quickly allows you to choose the right pattern immediately — before you even start speaking.

Question Type Signal Words Best Patterns
Opinion Do you think…? / What is your view…? / Should…? P1, P2, P10
Comparison How does X compare to Y? / Is X different from Y? P4, P8
Cause & Effect Why do you think…? / What causes…? / What are the effects? P5, P3
Advantages/Disadvantages What are the benefits…? / What are the drawbacks…? P6, P7, P10
Hypothetical / Speculation What would happen if…? / Imagine a world where… P9, P2, P7
Past vs. Present How has X changed? / Is X different now compared to before? P8, P4
Future Predictions How do you think X will change? / What will happen to X? P9, P2
Social / Abstract To what extent…? / What role does X play in society? P3, P10, P5

Worked Examples by Question Type

Opinion Question

Question: Do you think social media has been good for society overall?

Using Pattern 1 (Direct Answer → Reason → Example)

I think it's had more negative than positive effects overall. The main reason is that while social media has connected people across distances, it has simultaneously created divisive echo chambers where people only encounter views that mirror their own. A clear example is the way misinformation spreads during election periods — it's not moderated effectively and it shapes public opinion in ways that weren't possible before.

Using Pattern 10 (Idea → Counterpoint → Balanced Conclusion)

There are genuine benefits — social movements like climate activism have gained real traction through social media, and people in isolated places can find communities that support them. However, the negative effects on mental health, political polarisation, and the spread of misinformation are hard to ignore. My feeling is that social media is a powerful tool, and like most powerful tools, it depends entirely on how it's used — and right now, the regulation and design of these platforms hasn't caught up with the harm they can cause.

Cause & Effect Question

Question: Why do you think obesity rates are rising in many countries?

Using Pattern 5 (Cause → Effect → Real-World Example)

I think the primary cause is the combination of ultra-processed food being cheap and aggressively marketed, alongside increasingly sedentary lifestyles. The effect is that people consume far more calories than they burn, almost without realising it. In the United States and UK, the rise in obesity has closely tracked the rise of fast food availability and desk-based work, which strongly suggests these are the main drivers.

Future Prediction Question

Question: How do you think cities will change in the next 50 years?

Using Pattern 9 (Present → Future Prediction → Justification)

Right now, cities are dealing with challenges like congestion, pollution, and housing shortages. I would predict that future cities will be considerably greener and more vertical — with more rooftop gardens, built-in renewable energy systems, and far fewer personal vehicles. The justification is that both economic pressure and climate targets are going to force urban planners to rethink how space is used, and we're already seeing early versions of this in cities like Singapore and Amsterdam.

Hypothetical / Speculation Question

Question: What would happen if schools abolished homework?

Using Pattern 7 (Problem → Solution → Outcome)

Well, the problem homework is meant to solve is reinforcing learning outside the classroom. If it were abolished, there would probably need to be more focused in-class practice time and better study skills taught directly. The outcome might actually be positive — research suggests that excessive homework causes stress without significantly improving results, so replacing it with more effective classroom methods could actually raise achievement levels while reducing student anxiety.

Development Techniques

How to Extend Answers Naturally

Short answers are the most common problem in Part 3. Every answer needs to be built, not simply stated. The techniques below are the five most effective ways to expand any answer naturally — each one adds a different dimension to what you're saying.

🔑
Key principle

You don't need to use all five techniques in one answer. Choose one or two, apply them naturally, and your answer will already feel much more complete.

Technique 1: Add a Reason

The simplest and most important extension technique. After making a statement, ask yourself: Why is this true?

What to say: "…because…" / "The reason is that…" / "This is largely due to…"

❌ Too Short

"I think people should exercise more."

✓ Extended

"I think people should exercise more, largely because modern lifestyles have made physical activity almost completely optional — most of us sit at desks for eight hours a day and drive everywhere. Our bodies simply aren't designed for that level of inactivity."

What changed: A reason (why exercise matters) was added, then it was made concrete by describing the modern sedentary lifestyle.

Technique 2: Add an Example

Examples make abstract ideas real. They show the examiner that you can connect general ideas to the real world.

What to say: "For example…" / "Take, for instance…" / "A good example of this is…" / "We can see this in…"

❌ Too Short

"Technology has changed the way people learn."

✓ Extended

"Technology has fundamentally changed the way people learn. Take platforms like Coursera or Khan Academy — a student in a rural village can now access the same university-level content as someone at an elite institution. That kind of equalisation of access simply wasn't possible twenty years ago."

What changed: A specific platform example made the general claim feel real and grounded.

Technique 3: Add a Consequence

Explain what happens as a result of something. This shows analytical depth.

What to say: "As a result…" / "This means that…" / "The consequence is…" / "This leads to…"

❌ Too Short

"When people don't sleep enough, their health suffers."

✓ Extended

"When people don't sleep enough, their health suffers. As a result, we see higher rates of obesity, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease among people who consistently sleep fewer than six hours. This means that addressing sleep deprivation isn't just a personal choice — it's becoming a public health priority."

What changed: A consequence chain was added — the answer moved from a statement to a linked sequence of outcomes.

Technique 4: Add a Contrast

Introduce an opposing idea or exception. This shows nuanced thinking and avoids oversimplification.

What to say: "That said…" / "However…" / "On the other hand…" / "Although…" / "While this is true for many, it's worth noting that…"

❌ Too Short

"University degrees are still valuable."

✓ Extended

"University degrees are still valuable in many fields — medicine, law, and engineering are obvious examples. That said, in areas like tech and entrepreneurship, practical skills and self-directed learning are increasingly more valued than formal qualifications. So the picture is quite nuanced, and it really depends on the industry."

What changed: A contrast was introduced after the initial claim, showing the speaker can think beyond a single perspective.

Technique 5: Add a Condition (if / when / unless)

Introduce a hypothetical or conditional scenario. This adds sophistication and shows you can think about variables.

What to say: "If that happens…" / "Provided that…" / "Unless governments act…" / "In the event that…"

❌ Too Short

"Electric cars will become more common."

✓ Extended

"Electric cars will certainly become more common over the next decade. However, that growth really depends on whether the charging infrastructure keeps up — if people don't have convenient, affordable places to charge their vehicles, uptake will remain slower than expected. Provided governments and private companies invest adequately in that infrastructure, I think the transition could happen quite quickly."

What changed: A condition was introduced that qualifies the prediction, adding realism and demonstrating higher-level thinking.

Flexibility Training

One Question, Multiple Approaches

The key to real fluency is not having one fixed answer — it's knowing that any question can be approached in several different ways depending on your ideas in that moment. The exercises below show you how the same question can produce very different, equally strong answers depending on which pattern you choose.

Question: "Do you think it's important to learn a foreign language?"

Approach A — Pattern 1 (Direct Answer → Reason → Example)
I do think it's genuinely important, yes. The main reason is that language shapes how you think — when you learn another language, you don't just learn words, you gain access to a different way of perceiving the world. A good example is how Japanese has specific words for concepts that don't exist in English, like ma — the idea of meaningful silence. Learning that kind of vocabulary shifts your perspective in a way that's hard to achieve otherwise.
Approach B — Pattern 10 (Idea → Counterpoint → Balanced Conclusion)
On one level, with translation apps becoming increasingly powerful, you could argue the practical need to learn foreign languages is declining. However, there's so much more to language than translation — cultural empathy, building genuine relationships, and the cognitive benefits of bilingualism. On balance, I'd say learning languages remains highly important, especially for young people, because the benefits go well beyond communication.
Approach C — Pattern 2 (Opinion → Explanation → Result)
I believe it's extremely important. When you speak another language, you signal respect for another culture — you've invested time in understanding the world from their perspective. The result is often a much deeper level of trust and connection, whether in business, friendship, or diplomacy. Countries that encourage multilingualism tend to have more successful international relationships for exactly this reason.

What's different? Approach A is factual and example-heavy. Approach B shows critical thinking and balance. Approach C focuses on consequences. All three are strong, natural answers to the same question.

Question: "How has technology changed the way people work?"

Approach A — Pattern 8 (Present → Past Comparison → Reason)
Work today is almost unrecognisably different from even thirty years ago. Previously, a job meant being in a physical office for fixed hours, with communication happening by post or phone. Now, someone can manage a global team entirely remotely, respond instantly to a colleague in another time zone, and complete tasks using AI tools that didn't exist a decade ago. The change has been driven by the combination of broadband internet, cloud computing, and smartphones all arriving within the same generation.
Approach B — Pattern 5 (Cause → Effect → Real-World Example)
The cause is really the rapid spread of affordable digital tools — communication platforms, project management software, and AI assistants. The effect is a dramatic shift in what employers expect: workers are now expected to be constantly reachable, highly adaptable, and able to manage their own time without supervision. In companies like Spotify and Airbnb, this has led to fully remote or "work from anywhere" policies that would have seemed radical just a decade ago.

What's different? Approach A takes a historical perspective and shows change over time. Approach B analyses the mechanism of change and its effects. Both are equally valid and both score well.

Question: "What responsibilities do wealthy countries have towards poorer ones?"

Approach A — Pattern 7 (Problem → Solution → Outcome)
The core problem is that global inequality creates instability — poverty, conflict, and climate vulnerability in one region don't stay contained there. A realistic solution is for wealthy nations to allocate a meaningful percentage of GDP to development aid, but more importantly, to reform trade rules that currently favour wealthier economies. If that happened, the outcome would be a more economically balanced world where fewer people are driven to extreme circumstances.
Approach B — Pattern 10 (Idea → Counterpoint → Balanced Conclusion)
There's a strong moral case that countries which have historically benefited from colonialism or global trade imbalances have a responsibility to give back. However, foreign aid is a complicated issue — poorly designed aid can create dependency and undermine local industries. On balance, I think the most responsible approach isn't charity but structural support: fair trade agreements, debt relief, and investment in education and infrastructure rather than direct handouts.

What's different? Approach A is solution-oriented and practical. Approach B is more reflective and nuanced, showing awareness of complexity. Both demonstrate sophisticated Part 3 thinking.

Troubleshooting

Common Speaking Problems & Practical Fixes

Every learner experiences specific speaking problems. The key is to recognise yours, understand why it happens, and apply a targeted fix. Study each problem carefully and identify which ones apply to you.

⚠️ Problem 1: Answers Are Too Short
Why It Happens

The learner answers the question literally — they hear "Do you think X?" and simply say "Yes, I think X." This is a habit from everyday conversation, where short answers are normal. In Part 3, however, the examiner needs you to develop your ideas.

Simple Fix Strategy

After your first sentence, mentally ask yourself: "Why? What example? What happens as a result?" Train yourself to never stop at one sentence. Use the 1+3 rule: one statement, plus at least three of these — a reason, an example, a consequence, a contrast, or a condition.

❌ Before

"Yes, I think the internet has changed education a lot."

✓ After

"Yes, I think the internet has transformed education in really profound ways. For example, a student in a remote area can now access lectures from MIT or Oxford for free, which was completely unthinkable thirty years ago. The result is a democratisation of learning — but only for those with reliable internet access, which isn't everyone yet."

⚠️ Problem 2: Repetition of Basic Vocabulary
Why It Happens

Under pressure, learners default to the words they know best — "good," "bad," "important," "nice," "a lot." This happens because the brain prioritises speed and safety when anxious.

Simple Fix Strategy

Prepare topic-specific vocabulary banks before your exam. For each major topic (technology, environment, work, etc.), learn 10–15 more precise alternatives for common words. In practice, slow down slightly and give yourself a moment to retrieve the richer word.

⚠️ Problem 3: "I Don't Know" Moments
Why It Happens

The learner encounters a question about a topic they haven't considered before, panics, and freezes. This is common with abstract or philosophical questions.

Simple Fix Strategy

You never need to actually know the "right answer." Your job is to think out loud. Use buying time phrases to give yourself a moment, then start with the most obvious thing that comes to mind and build from there using a pattern.

Buying Time Phrases

  • "That's an interesting question — let me think about that for a moment."
  • "I haven't thought about this in exactly this way before, but off the top of my head…"
  • "Well, there are a few ways to look at this…"
  • "I'm not entirely sure, but I think the key point here is…"
  • "That's quite a complex issue. I'd probably start by saying…"
❌ Before

"I don't know. It's very complicated. I can't answer that."

✓ After

"That's quite a complex question — let me think. Well, I'd probably say that governments have the primary responsibility here, because individuals can only do so much on their own. Without policy and infrastructure, personal action has limited impact."

⚠️ Problem 4: Overthinking Before Speaking
Why It Happens

Learners try to plan a perfect, complete answer in their head before saying anything. This creates long silences and internal pressure. In spoken English, this level of pre-planning is unnatural.

Simple Fix Strategy

Use the "Start and Build" method. Open your answer with a simple sentence, then let the pattern guide you. You don't need to know where you're going before you start — you just need to know the first step. Trust the pattern to carry you forward.

Starting sentence only — then building using Pattern 1
"I think access to healthcare should be a basic right…" [→ Reason: because health affects a person's ability to participate fully in society] [→ Example: countries like Norway or Canada that have universal systems tend to have higher productivity and lower inequality overall]
⚠️ Problem 5: Translating from L1 (First Language)
Why It Happens

The learner constructs the answer in their native language first, then translates. This causes unnatural phrasing, wrong collocations, and slow delivery.

Simple Fix Strategy

Shift your thinking to English immediately by using pattern openers as a bridge. Instead of translating a thought, start with an English phrase you know and let your idea follow it. Practice "thinking in English" by doing daily micro-conversations with yourself in English — describe what you see, react to the news, or argue both sides of a simple issue out loud.

✅
Daily Practice Tip

Every day, pick one Part 3 topic and speak about it for 60 seconds in English — even if no one is listening. Start with "Personally, I think…" and build from there. After a week, this thinking-in-English habit begins to feel automatic.

1 Do you think people today read less than they used to?
Pattern 8 + Contrast
I think the type of reading has changed rather than the amount. Pattern 1 + Consequence
Yes, I think some regulation is both reasonable and necessary. The reason is that advertising shapes behaviour, especially in children, and unhealthy food is disproportionately targeted at young audiences. As a consequence, restricting advertising of high-sugar or high-fat products — particularly during children's programming — would likely contribute to healthier diets over time without restricting adult consumer choice.

Pattern 10
Cities clearly offer a concentration of services and opportunities that rural areas can't match. My view is that cities and countryside are interdependent — we can't sustain one without the other.

Pattern 5
The primary mechanism is that advertising creates desire for things people didn't previously feel they needed. Pattern 2
I think it's more important than ever, precisely because it's become rarer. When people have strong community ties, they experience lower rates of depression, higher resilience, and a greater sense of purpose. Pattern 8 + Hedging
Significant progress has been made — in many countries, women have equal legal rights, educational access, and professional opportunities that simply didn't exist a century ago. Pattern 10

My position is that it should be permitted only where strictly necessary, heavily regulated, and actively replaced as alternatives become viable.

Pattern 8

People volunteer for a surprisingly wide range of reasons. For some, it's about a sense of purpose — feeling that they're contributing to something larger than their own daily life. Generally speaking, the motivation tends to be a mix of altruism and self-interest, and that's not a contradiction — the most powerful community action tends to be where personal fulfilment and social good overlap.

Q1 Why do you think some people feel more connected to their local community than others?
Pattern 5 — Cause → Effect → Real-World Example I think a lot of it comes down to how long someone has lived in a place and whether they have personal ties there — family, long-standing friendships, or shared history with the area. When those ties exist, people feel invested. But when someone has just moved in, or lives in a transient neighbourhood where everyone is a stranger, it's very difficult to feel that sense of belonging. We see this most clearly in cities like London, where resident turnover is high and many people genuinely don't know their neighbours despite living in close proximity for years.
Q2 Do you think local governments do enough to support community life?
Pattern 1 — Direct Answer → Reason → Example Honestly, I think in most places they could do considerably more. The reason is that community infrastructure — things like parks, community centres, local libraries, and public events — tends to be the first thing cut when budgets are reduced, yet these are precisely the spaces that allow people to mix and form bonds. For example, in areas where libraries and youth centres have been closed, researchers have documented corresponding rises in antisocial behaviour and social isolation, which suggests these spaces matter far more than their cost might suggest.
Q3 How has urbanisation affected the way people relate to the communities around them?
Pattern 8 — Present → Past Comparison → Reason In urban environments today, the relationship between people and community is much more fragmented than it was for previous generations. In smaller, pre-industrial communities, people relied on each other for survival — shared resources, childcare, food production — and that created deep, organic bonds. Now, in cities, people are largely economically self-sufficient and can meet most of their needs anonymously, which removes the structural incentive to form close community ties. The density of cities creates proximity without connection.
Q4 Do you think technology could help bring communities together, or does it create more division?
Pattern 10 — Idea → Counterpoint → Balanced Conclusion Technology definitely has the potential to strengthen communities — local Facebook groups, neighbourhood apps like Nextdoor, and online forums have helped people organise events, share information, and support each other in practical ways. However, the same tools can also create division — they amplify local grievances, spread misinformation, and give a disproportionate voice to the loudest or most confrontational members of a group. On balance, I think technology is a useful supplement to community life, but it can't substitute for face-to-face interaction — which is where real trust and belonging are built.
Q5 What do you think individuals can do to make their communities stronger?
Pattern 7 — Problem → Solution → Outcome The core problem is that most people are waiting for someone else to create the community they want to live in. A simple but powerful solution is to act as a connector — organise a street event, introduce yourself to a neighbour, share resources, or volunteer with a local organisation. If even a small percentage of people in a neighbourhood took those steps, the cumulative effect would be a significantly more cohesive and resilient community — one where people feel they belong and are looked after.
Q6 Should wealthier members of a community have a greater responsibility to support others?
Pattern 10 — Idea → Counterpoint → Balanced Conclusion There's a compelling argument that those with more resources — whether financial, professional, or social — should contribute more to the communities that have supported their success. It's not just about charity; it's about recognising that wealth is rarely created entirely independently. On the other hand, making community contribution feel like an obligation can breed resentment and undermine the goodwill that makes it effective. I think the better approach is fostering a culture where giving back is seen as a social norm and a source of meaning — something people genuinely want to do rather than feel forced into.
Q7 How do you think the idea of community will change in the next few decades?
Pattern 9 — Present → Future Prediction → Justification At present, community is still primarily geographic — we belong to communities defined by where we live. My prediction is that over the next few decades, "community" will increasingly be defined by shared values and interests rather than shared location. Online spaces already allow people with niche interests to form genuinely meaningful communities across continents. The justification is that as remote working, digital life, and global mobility continue to grow, the accident of geography will matter less than the intentional choice of who you connect with and why.
Q8 Is it realistic to expect a strong sense of community in large, diverse cities?
Pattern 3 — General Statement → Specific Example + Condition I think it's realistic, but it requires deliberate effort and good urban design. Large, diverse cities can absolutely sustain strong community life — the key is creating physical and social spaces where mixing happens naturally. Take the neighbourhood of Brixton in London, or the Marais district in Paris — both are diverse, dense urban areas with strong local identities and active community life. These examples show it's possible, provided the city invests in shared infrastructure and doesn't allow commercial development to hollow out the local character.
