Chapter 1

Why Story Structure Matters

Fluency

Stay Fluent

A clear structure removes hesitation. When you know what comes next, your speech flows naturally without long pauses or fillers.

Organisation

Stay Organised

Structure keeps your ideas logical and easy to follow. The examiner can track your response from beginning to end without confusion.

Time

Fill 2 Minutes

A formula gives you layers to expand on — situation, details, feelings, reflection — so you consistently reach the full time without rambling.

Naturalness

Sound Natural

Good structure prevents you from memorising scripts. You follow a framework and speak spontaneously within it — exactly what examiners reward.

Vocabulary

Show Range

When you're not thinking about what to say next, you can focus on how to say it — choosing richer vocabulary and more varied grammar.

Confidence

Speak Confidently

Knowing your structure means you walk into Part 2 with a plan. That confidence comes through in your tone and delivery.

Chapter 2

The 4 Story Structures

Choose one structure per answer depending on the question type. Each structure is designed to match a specific kind of topic so your story feels natural, not forced.

Structure 1

SPES

Situation · Problem · Effect · Solution

Best for: challenges, achievements, difficult situations

S
Situation Provide background context — when, where, who, what
P
Problem Describe what went wrong or the central challenge
E
Effect What happened as a result + how you felt
S
Solution How you solved it or what you learned from it
Mini Example "I once helped my cousin finish a school project. The problem was she lost her data. It made her extremely stressed, so I helped her reorganise everything. Luckily, we finished on time."
Structure 2

5W + Feeling + Future

Who · What · Where · When · Why · Feeling · Future

Best for: simple experiences, daily activities, people, items

5W
Who / What / Where / When / Why Establish all the basic facts of the experience
F
Feeling How did you feel? Be specific — not just "happy" or "good"
F
Future What does this mean for you going forward?
Mini Example "Last month I met a friendly Japanese tourist at the airport. We talked about food, and it made me feel relaxed. In the future, I'd like to talk to more travellers."
Structure 3

PDR

Past · Details · Reflection

Best for: childhood memories, travel, celebrations, people

P
Past Summary A brief overview of the memory or experience
D
2–3 Details Vivid, specific details that bring the memory to life
R
Reflection Why is it memorable? What did it mean to you?
Mini Example "When I was a child, I often visited my grandparents' village. I remember the smell of fresh bread and playing outside with my cousins. Looking back, that place shaped my personality."
Structure 4

Zoom In – Zoom Out

Overview → Vivid Details → Final Thought

Best for: long descriptions, scenery, journeys, events

↗
Zoom Out — Short Overview Set the scene broadly: who, what, where
🔍
Zoom In — 2–3 Vivid Details Describe specific sights, sounds, moments, or feelings
↗
Zoom Out — Final Thought A reflective closing statement on why it matters
Mini Example "One unforgettable trip was a train ride to the mountains. I saw fields, villages, and misty hills. Thinking about it now, the journey itself was the highlight."

Chapter 3

Part 2 Answer Formula

Every Part 2 answer — regardless of topic — follows the same five-part structure. Use this as your master template.

1
Opening — Paraphrase the Topic Restate the cue card prompt in your own words. Don't read it directly. This signals you've understood the task.
2
Background — When, Where, Who Establish the context quickly in 2–3 sentences. This is your "newspaper summary."
3
Main Story — Use 1 of the 4 Structures This is the heart of your answer. Choose SPES, 5W+F+F, PDR, or Zoom In–Out based on the topic type.
4
Extra Detail — A Mini-Problem or Vivid Moment Add one small challenge or sensory detail. This separates good answers from great ones.
5
Reflection — What You Learned / Why It Matters End with a "Golden Ending" line. This is what examiners specifically listen for.

✨ Golden Endings

Use one of these lines to close every answer naturally:

"That's why it's memorable to me." "It taught me a simple lesson." "It really changed my perspective." "I still think about it sometimes."

Chapter 4

The Layering Technique

Use this technique to extend any short statement into a full, rich sentence chain. One statement can become five layers — each one adding depth and vocabulary range.

Statement Make a simple claim
Reason Explain why
Example Give a specific case
Feeling Add emotion
Reflection Step back & reflect
💡
Full Example
"It was tiring [Statement] → because the bus broke down [Reason] → we waited an hour in the heat [Example] → I felt quite annoyed [Feeling] → but I learned to be patient in unexpected situations [Reflection]."

Super Summary

The 3 Hidden Requirements

No matter what the topic type is, examiners always expect these three things. If any are missing, the score drops.

1

Clear Narrative Line

Beginning → Middle → End. Your answer must feel like a complete story, not a list of facts.

2

Personal Response

Feelings, opinions, and reactions. The examiner needs to hear you, not just what happened.

3

Reflection

Why is this important to you? Every answer must have a clear "so what" — your takeaway or lesson.

📰
Advanced Technique

The Newspaper Method + SBAR

Combine the Newspaper Method (for your introduction) with the SBAR storytelling framework (for your main story). Together, they always give you a 1:45–2:00 minute answer.

⭐
The Master Formula
Headline → Summary → Setup → Build-Up → Action (3 details) → Reflection

Part 1 — The Newspaper Method (Introduction)

Step 1

Headline

One clear sentence showing exactly what you'll talk about.

"Today I'd like to describe a memorable trip I took last year."
Step 2

Summary

One sentence covering the basics: when, where, who.

"It happened last summer when I travelled to a small town with a close friend."
Step 3

Main Story

This is where SBAR takes over. The Newspaper intro takes only 5–10 seconds.

Part 2 — The SBAR Framework (Your Story)

S

Setup — Follow this exact order: When → Where → Who → What

Establish all the context before anything else. This anchors the examiner in your story.

"It was around June last year, in the northern part of the country, and I was travelling with my friend. We had planned a short weekend trip."
B

Build-Up — The inciting incident: how the story began

Tell the examiner what made the situation interesting or different. Choose one trigger: surprise, problem, change, weather, delay, or mistake.

"The trip became memorable when the scenery changed dramatically as the train left the city."
A

Action — Exactly 3 clear details, like camera shots in a film

Each detail should be a small, vivid moment. Don't rush through them — they're the heart of your answer.

① "The fields and rivers looked beautiful from the window." ② "A local vendor came on board selling homemade snacks." ③ "My friend and I had a deep conversation about our goals."
R

Reflection — Why it was memorable or meaningful

Examiners love this part because it completes the story with personal insight. This is what separates Band 6 from Band 7+.

"It was memorable because it helped me slow down and appreciate peaceful moments during travel."
⏱️
Strategy

Plan Your Answer in 1 Minute

Your goal is NOT to write sentences. Your goal is to write 8–12 keywords that help you speak confidently for 2 minutes.

💡
The Note-Taking Strategy
Write the skeleton, the triggers, and the memory anchors. The story comes naturally when you start speaking.
Newspaper Notes

Intro Keywords

Headline: 1 word — the topic (e.g., "Trip", "Gift", "Friend")

Summary: 3 words — WHEN / WHERE / WHO
e.g., "last yr / beach / cousin"

SBAR Notes

Story Keywords

  • S: 4 keywords — When / Where / Who / What
  • B: 1 keyword — the trigger (e.g., "delay", "surprise")
  • A: 3 keywords — three vivid details
  • R: 1 keyword — your reflection (e.g., "lesson", "calm")

Example — Planning a "Trip" Answer

Your Notes (Keywords Only)
Headline: Trip
Summary: last summer / mountains / friend
S: June / north / friend / weekend trip
B: scenery change
A: fields + river / vendor / deep chat
R: slow down
Checklist
  • ☐ Clear headline written?
  • ☐ When / Where / Who in 3 words?
  • ☐ Setup follows When → Where → Who → What?
  • ☐ One simple trigger for Build-Up?
  • ☐ Exactly 3 action details?
  • ☐ One reflection keyword?
  • ☐ Total: 8–12 keywords only?
  • ☐ Done in under 60 seconds?
🎙️
Performance

Delivery Notes

Pronunciation and fluency are separate from content. A great story delivered poorly still costs marks. Follow these principles.

⏸️
Pause Naturally Pauses at transitions sound confident, not hesitant. Don't fill them with "um" or "uh."
🎯
Emphasise Key Words Stress the important words in each sentence to guide the examiner's attention.
🐢
Slow Down at Transitions When moving from one part to the next, slow your pace slightly to signal the shift.
🚫
Don't Memorise A memorised answer sounds robotic. Speak from keywords, not from a script.
😌
Sound Relaxed Confidence in tone is rewarded. Breathe before you start, then speak as if telling a friend.
✅
Sound Natural Aim for a conversational register — not overly formal, not too casual.
🛡️
Safety

Handling Difficult or Personal Topics

Sometimes a cue card feels too personal or asks about something you haven't experienced. These lines give you a graceful way to redirect.

Too Personal

"I'd prefer not to talk about something too personal, so I'll describe a similar situation."

Haven't Experienced It

"I haven't experienced this exactly, but I can imagine a scenario that relates to it."

Sensitive Topic

"Let me talk about something mild that happened to me that relates to this."

Believable, Safe Story Checklist

✅Keep the story small and relatable
✅Add 1–2 sensory details
✅Add a mild problem (optional)
✅Use a simple, clear timeline
✅Avoid sensitive, political, traumatic topics
✅Always end with reflection

👤 People

A Person Who Influenced You

Cue Card 1 — Describe a person who has influenced your life: who they are, how you know them, what they did, and why their influence matters.
PDR
I'd like to talk about my high-school English teacher, Ms. Santos, who had a significant influence on my attitude toward learning.

In the past, I was actually quite shy and rarely volunteered to speak in class, especially in English. She noticed this and encouraged me slowly, in a very patient and gentle way.

One detail I remember clearly is that she never corrected students harshly. Instead, she would repeat our sentences in a more natural way, so it felt like a friendly suggestion rather than a mistake. Another detail that stands out is how she used stories, music, and short films to make lessons feel like something to look forward to, rather than something to fear.

Looking back, her influence matters to me because it helped me become more confident expressing myself. Without her support, I don't think I would have developed the courage to study languages seriously or pursue opportunities where communication plays a major role. Even today, I still apply the things she taught me about confidence, patience, and empathy.

A Person You Recently Met

Cue Card 2 — Describe someone you met recently and found interesting: who, where, what you talked about, and why they were interesting.
5W + Future
A few months ago, I met a Japanese software engineer named Kenta during a long layover at an airport lounge. We happened to sit at the same table, and since we both looked tired and bored, we started a casual conversation.

We talked about travel, food, and eventually technology, which is his field. He explained, in a very simple way, how artificial intelligence is being used to make daily life more convenient in Japan, especially in customer service. What impressed me was that he didn't speak like a typical engineer — he used everyday examples, like convenience store checkout systems and home appliances, which made the topic surprisingly easy to understand.

I felt comfortable talking to him because he was humble, friendly, and genuinely curious about my culture as well. In the future, I'd love to visit Japan and maybe even meet him again, because conversations like that remind me how much we can learn from strangers if we're open enough to talk.

📍 Places

A Quiet Place You Enjoy

Cue Card 3 — Describe a quiet place you enjoy visiting: where it is, how you found it, what you do there, and why you enjoy it.
Zoom
One quiet place I really enjoy visiting is a small riverside park about fifteen minutes from my home. It's not very well-known, so it never gets crowded. I discovered it accidentally while jogging in my neighbourhood during the pandemic.

When you arrive, the first thing you notice is the sound of flowing water. There are a few wooden benches facing the river, and tall trees that create a natural canopy. I often sit there with a cup of coffee, read a book, or simply watch the water moving slowly. One small detail I love is how the sunlight reflects on the river in the late afternoon — it creates this warm, golden glow that feels incredibly peaceful.

Whenever I spend time there, I feel refreshed and mentally lighter. Thinking about it now, I realise I enjoy this place because it gives me a chance to disconnect from noise, messages, and responsibilities. It acts like a mini-retreat right in the city, and that's why it has become one of my favourite spots.

A Place That Left a Strong Impression

Cue Card 4 — Describe a place you visited that left a strong impression on you: where it was, when, what it looked like, and why it impressed you.
Zoom
A few years ago, I visited an old European-style library during a work trip, and it left a strong impression on me. From the outside, it looked like an ordinary historical building, but once you stepped inside, the atmosphere completely changed.

The main hall had extremely high ceilings, tall wooden bookshelves, and stained-glass windows that filtered colourful light into the room. There were long wooden tables where people studied in complete silence. One detail that fascinated me was the smell of old books mixed with polished wood — it gave the whole place a sense of history and wisdom.

The reason this place impressed me so much is that it felt like stepping into a different century. Everything — from the architecture to the quiet, respectful environment — encouraged deep thinking. It made me appreciate how knowledge is preserved over generations, and it reminded me of the value of slow, focused learning.

🎁 Objects

A Useful Object You Use Daily

Cue Card 5 — Describe a useful object you use every day: what it is, how long you've had it, what you use it for, and why it's useful.
5W
One object I use every single day is my wireless earbuds, which I bought about two years ago. I originally purchased them because I wanted something lightweight for commuting, but they ended up becoming one of the most helpful items I own.

I use them for listening to music, taking calls, and watching short videos while waiting in line. They're extremely comfortable, and the sound quality blocks out most background noise. What I appreciate most is that they allow me to enjoy small pockets of learning throughout the day — things like listening to language podcasts or short lectures.

I feel attached to them not because they are expensive, but because they help me stay productive and relaxed in stressful environments. Without them, commuting and long waiting times would feel much more tiring. So, for me, they're not just a gadget; they're a daily source of comfort and convenience.

A Gift You Received

Cue Card 6 — Describe a gift you received that was meaningful: what it was, who gave it, when, and why it was meaningful.
PDR
A meaningful gift I received was a handmade sketchbook from a close friend about three years ago. At the time, I was interested in drawing but wasn't confident enough to take it seriously.

One detail that made the gift special was that she designed the cover herself using pressed flowers and watercolour illustrations. Another detail was the inscription on the first page — she wrote a simple message telling me to "create without fear," which felt incredibly supportive.

Reflecting on it now, the sketchbook mattered because it encouraged me to start drawing more regularly. It showed that someone believed in my ability before I even believed in myself. Even though I've filled all the pages, I still keep it as a reminder that small acts of encouragement can have a lasting impact.

🎉 Events

A Cultural Event You Enjoyed

Cue Card 7 — Describe a cultural event you attended: what, where, when, what happened, and why you enjoyed it.
Zoom
A cultural event I really enjoyed was a lantern festival I attended two years ago during a visit to a nearby province. It was held in an open field near a lake, and people from different communities participated.

The moment that stood out most was the lantern-lighting ceremony. Hundreds of people lit their lanterns at the same time, and as they released them into the night sky, the whole area glowed with warm, floating lights. There were also traditional performances and food stalls, but the lanterns were definitely the highlight.

I enjoyed the event because it created a sense of unity among complete strangers. Even though I didn't know most of the people there, the shared experience felt meaningful. The quiet beauty of seeing the lanterns drift upward made me appreciate the simplicity and power of collective traditions.

An Event That Didn't Go as Planned

Cue Card 8 — Describe an event you attended that didn't go as planned: what it was, what went wrong, how people reacted, and what you learned.
SPES
Last year, I attended a friend's outdoor birthday picnic, and although we were all excited, things didn't go exactly as planned. The situation seemed perfect at first — the weather was sunny, the grass was dry, and everyone brought food to share.

However, about an hour into the picnic, dark clouds suddenly appeared. The problem was that none of us had checked the weather forecast. Within minutes, it started raining heavily. People scrambled to pack their things, but many items got soaked, and the cake was ruined.

Even though it was chaotic, something unexpected happened: instead of getting upset, everyone just started laughing. We ended up moving to a nearby shelter, ordering takeout, and celebrating in a more relaxed way.

The experience taught me that sometimes imperfect moments become the most memorable. It reminded me of the importance of flexibility and keeping a positive attitude when things don't go according to plan.

🎯 Activities

A Hobby You Enjoy

Cue Card 9 — Describe a hobby you enjoy doing: what it is, how often, why you enjoy it, and how it affects your life.
5W
A hobby I really enjoy is journalling, which I usually do three to four times a week. I started doing it during a stressful period because I needed a simple way to organise my thoughts.

Most of the time, I write in the evening with a cup of tea. I jot down what happened during the day, how I felt, and what I'm grateful for. One thing I enjoy is that there are no rules — I can write freely without worrying about grammar or structure.

This hobby affects my life in a positive way because it helps me process emotions and reduce stress. Whenever I look back at older entries, I can see how much I've grown over time. It reminds me that progress isn't always loud or dramatic — sometimes it happens quietly in the background.

An Activity You Learned Recently

Self-Study

Practice Drills

Repeat these drills daily until the techniques become automatic. Start with writing, then move to speaking, then recording.

1 SBAR Mini-Stories 5 minutes
Choose one prompt and write a 4-sentence SBAR story. Do all 5 for a full session.

Prompts:
  • A time you arrived late
  • A surprising message you received
  • A time you bought something useful
  • A conversation you enjoyed
  • A simple mistake you made

Example (for "bought something useful"): S: Last month, at a mall, I bought a small planner. B: I chose it because my schedule was getting disorganised. A: It had a clean layout, a habit tracker, and monthly goals pages. R: It helped me stay organised — a great purchase.
2 Newspaper Headlines 3 minutes
Write only the headline sentence for each of these topics:

  • A person who helped you
  • A happy childhood memory
  • A place you enjoy visiting
  • A book or film you liked
  • A difficult task you completed

Example: "Today I'd like to describe a place I love visiting in my free time."
3 Newspaper Summaries 5 minutes
Take the 5 headlines from Drill 2. Add one sentence summary (when / where / who) to each.

Example: "It happened two years ago when I visited a quiet beach with my cousin."
4 3-Detail Challenge 5 minutes
Choose any topic. Write exactly 3 action details — like camera shots. Keep them vivid and specific.

Example Topic — A Trip:
  • The bus passed through green rice fields.
  • I tried a snack I'd never eaten before.
  • A child sitting next to me shared a story about her hometown.
5 Reflection Builder 3 minutes
Complete each sentence with a natural, personal reflection:

  • "It was memorable because…"
  • "I learned that…"
  • "It changed me by…"
  • "It made me realise…"
  • "This experience mattered because…"

For each cue card below, complete all 3 rounds.

  • Too dramatic → make the problem milder

Assessment

Self-Evaluation Checklist

Use this checklist after every practice session. Honest self-assessment is the fastest way to improve.

⭐ Section A — Newspaper Method (Intro)
I gave a clear headline sentence.
I gave a 1-sentence summary (when, where, who).
My introduction was 5–10 seconds only — not too long.
⭐ Section B — SBAR Structure
SETUP: I followed "when → where → who → what."
BUILD-UP: I explained how the event started.
ACTION: I gave exactly three clear details.
REFLECTION: I explained why it was memorable or meaningful.
I avoided sensitive, traumatic, political, or overly dramatic content.
⭐ Section C — Time & Coherence
My answer lasted 1:45–2:00 minutes.
The story had a clear flow (no sudden jumps).
I did not pause for too long or freeze.
I did not speak too fast.
⭐ Section D — Language Use
I used past tenses correctly throughout.
My vocabulary was natural and not memorised.
I used 2–3 linking words (at first, afterwards, finally…).
I avoided overly complex or unnatural phrases.
⭐ Section E — Personal Reflection
I included my feelings at least once.
I included a personal lesson or message.
My story was believable, safe, and relatable.
🎖️
The Goal: A Calm, Natural Story
A good Part 2 answer feels like a calm, natural conversation — not a movie scene, not a memorised script, and not a biography. If you can check YES for most boxes above, your answer is examiner-ready.
